A Tale of Two Conferences

ACSW 2013 and NZOUG 2013

Nigel Stanger

Department of Information Science

10 May, 2013

SOURCE: ACSW / NZOUG

I had the opportunity earlier this year to attend both an academic and an industry conference in close succession, and it’s interesting to compare the differences between the two.

The two conferences were ACSW (AWC) 2013 in Adelaide and NZOUG 2013 in Wellington. Will probably spend more time on NZOUG as it was different from the normal academic conference, and also the one I had more direct involvement with.

ACSW

  • Started 1978 (UNSW)
  • (Five) times in NZ
    • Canterbury 1994

  • ACSW = Australasian Computer Science Week
  • CORE = Computing Research and Education
  • I’ve been to 2004, 2012, 2013 (presented Angrosh’s work at AWC).
  • Many of these conferences are very small, and some definitely wouldn't survive on their own.

NZOUG

  • Founded 1989 as part of ANZORA
  • Independent 1997
  • Forum for all Oracle users in NZ
    • Oracle (database, APEX, Collaboration Suite, E-Business Suite, Exalogic)
    • Sun (Java, Exadata, SPARC)
    • JD Edwards (ERP)
    • PeopleSoft (HR, financials, SCM, CRM)
    • Siebel (CRM)
  • Strong links with Oracle and international UGs
  • Historically more associated with technical roles like DBAs and application developers, but the acquisition of the latter three has significantly changed the playing field.
  • The latter three are currently being merged with E-Business Suite to produce Oracle Fusion Applications.

NZOUG conference

  • Industry conference aimed at NZ Oracle practitioners (i.e., non-academic)
  • Has been running since about 1998 (?)
  • Originally annual; every 18 months since 2005
  • Auckland, Hamilton, Rotorua, Wellington
  • 2013 (Wellington): ≈80 presentations; ≈250 delegates
  • Most of the presentations were either experience reports or educational in nature. Very few similar to typical academic papers (e.g., binary data types paper).
  • Smaller than ACSW, but not by a huge margin?
  • Mainly Auckland and Rotorua; 2013 was the first time in Wellington. This reflects the geographic distribution of the user base.

ACSW 2013: Interesting papers

Keynotes

  • Towards the end-to-end design for big data management in the cloud: Why, how, and when? (Divyakant Agrawal, UCSB)
  • Virtual and physical interactions: Connecting real and virtual worlds seamlessly (Maki Sugimoto, Keio University)

ACE

  • How difficult are exams? A framework for assessing the complexity of introductory programming exams
  • Agrawal talked about the problems of managing big data in cloud environments (distribution vs. consistency, inevitability of failure and how to recover, concurrent asynchronous access, testing).
  • Sugimoto talked about various non-visual augmented reality technologies developed in his lab (e.g., air movement for video conferencing).
  • Exams paper has 11 authors! Interesting point is that programming problems often assume students understand the domain context (e.g., what a car registration is, or the rules of a well-known game).

NZOUG 2013: Interesting presentations

  • 10 times better performance with BINARY data-types! (Paul Guerin, HP)
  • Tom’s top 12 things about the latest generation of database technology (Tom Kyte, Oracle)
  • Real World Performance Tour (Tom Kyte, Andrew Holdsworth & Graham Wood, Oracle)

The binary data types presentation was closest to a research paper I saw at NZOUG (experimental performance analysis). Conclusion: BINARY_FLOAT up to 10× faster than NUMBER, as it uses hardware FP instructions. You lose precision with IEEE 754 (38 decimal digits → 15–17), but 38 is mostly way overkill. Quoted 38 digits as “one atom in the diameter of the Universe”—turns out to be only two orders of magnitude off! (Atomic diameter ≈10-11–10-10, observable Universe 8.8 × 1026 / 1038 = 8.8 x 10-12.)

NZOUG 2013: New in Oracle 12c

SOURCE: Goran Ristic / NZOUG

  • Inline PL/SQL functions in SQL using WITH
  • Default to sequence, metadata defaults, default on explicit NULL
  • Row pattern matching with regexes
  • Easy top-n and paginated queries using FETCH and OFFSET
  • Mix reference and interval partitioning
  • Adaptive execution plans
  • Application continuity preserves transaction state across node failover
  • Pluggable databases (multiple databases share one SGA + processes)

NZOUG 2013: Real World Performance

SOURCE: Goran Ristic / NZOUG ↑     Nigel Stanger →

RWP ran entire second day, was so good I stayed for all of it. Attacked common misconceptions in database performance tuning by showing real examples running on an Exadata back at Oracle HQ (brave!). Custom dashboards showed everything in real time (connections, transaction throughput, memory usage, etc.). Would love to use their stuff for teaching, but don’t have the hardware! Highlights: “silos of hate” (everyone blames others for bad performance); non-linear catastrophic failure of system as connections exceed cores; “s/w is the new h/w” (can easily change the h/w but not the s/w!); loading ¼ billion rows into DW in ≈10 minutes (set-based vs. row-based processing).

The big differences

  • NZOUG used a PCO (Auaha)
  • NZOUG presentations accepted/rejected on abstract review; no “papers” as such
  • Many of the speakers were “big names” in the Oracle community
  • Whole-day streams alongside “normal” sessions (Real World Performance and JD Edwards)
  • Mobile app for NZOUG as well as paper programme (Gather Digital)
  • Sponsorship was critical and very obvious

The mobile app wasn’t cheap (about US$5000?), but was useful. Wasn’t used as widely as we’d hoped, but it’ll probably be more in demand next time as smartphone and tablet penetration increases. We were Gather Digital’s first contract outside the US (although ours was based on ODTUG’s app). Say more about architecture (thin native front end to a web-based service back end). Deploy front end to app stores once (after finalising design elements), then content is loaded into/edited on back end as needed.
